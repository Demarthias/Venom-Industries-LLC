THE HIDDEN COST
OF SAAS DEBT

Most SaaS companies don't realize they're paying a performance tax every single day. Bloated toolchains, unaudited dependencies, and zero error boundaries are quietly draining conversion rates and engineering velocity. Here's how to find it — and what it actually costs.

The term "tech debt" gets thrown around in every engineering retrospective. But there's a specific category of debt that's silently more expensive than most teams realize — what I call SaaS debt: the accumulated cost of shipping fast without shipping clean.

It shows up differently than traditional technical debt. It's not just legacy code or outdated architecture. It's the bloated analytics stack that fires 12 events per page load. The third-party script loaded synchronously in the <head> because nobody questioned the implementation from 2 years ago. The dependency tree that hasn't been audited since the MVP was shipped.

The reason it's so insidious is that it doesn't break anything. The site still loads. The app still works. But it's working worse every quarter — and the cost is buried in metrics most teams don't even track.

WHERE THE TAX GETS PAID

Conversion rate

Google's research is consistent: a 1-second delay in mobile page load reduces conversions by up to 20%. If your LCP is 4.5 seconds on mobile — which is common for SaaS marketing sites running multiple third-party scripts — you're leaving a significant portion of potential revenue on the floor every single day.

The compounding effect is worse. Slow pages rank lower in Google. Lower rankings mean less organic traffic. Less organic traffic means paid channels carry more of the load. And paid traffic is far more expensive than organic.

The math is simple: if your site converts at 3% and has 10,000 monthly visitors, a 1-second improvement in load time that lifts conversion to 3.6% generates 60 additional customers per month — before you spend a dollar on ads.

Engineering velocity

SaaS debt doesn't just cost money at the customer level. It costs time at the engineering level. When you have:

  • Components over 400 lines with mixed concerns
  • No TypeScript or incomplete PropTypes, so the IDE can't catch errors
  • Zero error boundaries, meaning a single render error white-screens the app
  • Outdated dependencies that other packages depend on, making upgrades cascading

...every new feature takes longer to ship. Not dramatically longer — just consistently slower. 10% longer per sprint compounds into a meaningful drag on your roadmap over a year.

Security exposure

Dependency CVEs are the most quantifiable form of SaaS debt. In a typical SaaS audit, we find 2–4 known vulnerabilities in production dependencies — packages that have had patches available for months that nobody installed because the update process is manual and it never made it onto the sprint board.

The most common example we see is lodash. Version 4.17.15 has a prototype pollution vulnerability (CVE-2021-23337, CVSS 7.2) that's been patchable since 2021. It's still in production on a significant percentage of codebases we audit. Not because anyone decided to leave it — because nobody checked.

Critical

lodash 4.17.15 — CVE-2021-23337

Prototype pollution vulnerability. CVSS score 7.2. Fix: update to 4.17.21 or later. Run npm audit fix.

High

Missing Content-Security-Policy header

No CSP set. Site exposed to XSS via injected scripts. Fix: add CSP header with appropriate directives for your stack.

Medium

No X-Frame-Options header

Site can be embedded in iframes on other domains — enables clickjacking. Fix: add X-Frame-Options: SAMEORIGIN.

HOW TO FIND IT

The good news is that SaaS debt is highly auditable. Unlike traditional tech debt, which requires deep knowledge of your codebase to quantify, most SaaS debt is detectable with standard tooling in under a day.

Performance

Run your site through PageSpeed Insights on mobile. Look at your LCP (Largest Contentful Paint), CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift), and FID/INP. If LCP is above 2.5 seconds on mobile — which it almost certainly is if you're running analytics, chat, and tag manager — you have a performance problem with a clear fix path.

# Run an audit in your terminal npx lighthouse https://yoursite.com --only-categories=performance # Or use the Chrome DevTools Lighthouse tab # Focus on: LCP, CLS, TBT, and the opportunities section

Security

Run npm audit in your project root. Run your URL through Mozilla Observatory. Both take under 2 minutes and will surface the majority of your security debt immediately.

Dependencies

Use npm outdated to see what's stale. Use npx depcheck to find packages that are installed but not actually used. Unused dependencies in your bundle are pure dead weight — they increase build size, slow installs, and expand your attack surface for no benefit.

WHAT TO DO WITH IT

The hardest part of SaaS debt isn't finding it — it's prioritizing it against feature work. Here's the framework we use in every audit:

P0 — Do immediately. Security vulnerabilities with known CVEs, anything breaking Core Web Vitals by more than 50% above threshold, and any unhandled error patterns that can crash the application. These have direct revenue or legal exposure.

P1 — This sprint. Performance improvements with clear ROI (image optimization, script deferral, caching headers). Security headers. These are typically 1–4 hour fixes with disproportionate impact.

P2 — Next quarter. Structural tech debt (component refactoring, TypeScript migration, testing coverage). High effort, important, but not urgent.

The most common mistake teams make is treating P0 and P1 items the same as P2 — putting them in the backlog and getting to them "eventually." Security CVEs and conversion-killing performance issues don't belong in the backlog. They belong in the next deploy.

THE BOTTOM LINE

SaaS debt is expensive precisely because it's invisible. It doesn't show up as an error in your logs or a spike in your alerting. It shows up as a conversion rate that's 0.5% lower than it should be, and a sprint velocity that's 10% slower than it was 18 months ago, and a security posture that's one dependency away from a headline.

The fix isn't a big rewrite or a dedicated quarter of cleanup. It's a systematic audit that tells you exactly what's costing you money, in what order to fix it, and what the expected impact is.
